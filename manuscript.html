Ongoing Housing stress and cognitive functions - manuscript

Author

Takashi Iida

Introduction

Housing is a recognized driver of health disparities (Swope and Hernández 2019). In the World Health Organization’s 2010 framework, housing was classified as an intermediary determinant of health—downstream from structural forces yet directly shaping health outcomes (Solar and Irwin 2010). Its three key elements; affordability, suitability, and security (Mansour et al. 2022), when unmet, expose residents to homelessness, housing instability, and foreclosure. Housing insecurity in the United States is widespread and growing: more than one third of community-living older Americans report at least one form of it, including poor affordability, poor quality, or poor neighborhood conditions (Paredes et al. 2024). In 2024, more than 700,000 people experienced homelessness—the highest count since 2007 (Sousa and Henry 2024). Older adults face particular vulnerability, and the population aged 65 and older is projected to grow rapidly in coming decades (Culhane et al., n.d.). Housing problems also harm communities: rising foreclosure rates erode local property tax revenues (Apgar, Duda, and Gorey 2005), and neighborhood eviction is linked to higher crime rates (Schwartz et al. 2024).

Housing problems generate chronic psychological stress (Talmatzky et al. 2026; Singh et al. 2019; Dotsikas et al. 2023), which contributes to several adverse health outcomes. Housing disadvantage is associated with poor mental health (Singh et al. 2019), depression (Kingsbury et al. 2018), and—for those who perceive a risk of eviction—anxiety and related medication use (Acharya, Bhatta, and Dhakal 2022). Housing instability also predicts adverse cardiometabolic outcomes (Gu, Faulkner, and Thorndike 2023).

Emerging evidence further suggests that housing stress may accelerate memory decline: foreclosure, for example, has been associated with faster cognitive decline among older adults (Kim and Schwartz 2024; Friedman et al. 2021). Cognitive decline in midlife and late life increases the risk of dementia and Alzheimer’s disease (Dhana et al. 2025; Belleville et al. 2017), making it important to identify modifiable contributing factors. Cognitive decline varies by domain; two domains especially relevant to Alzheimer’s disease are episodic memory and working memory. Episodic memory, assessed through immediate and delayed word recall, is an early cognitive marker with high diagnostic accuracy for Alzheimer’s disease (Weissberger et al. 2017). Working memory, which reflects attentional control and central executive function, is also commonly impaired in Alzheimer’s disease (Kirova, Bays, and Lagalwar 2015; Stopford et al. 2012). Studying these domains clarifies whether housing-related stressors contribute to dementia-relevant cognitive change.

The cognitive effects of housing stress may also differ by sex. Women score higher on immediate and delayed word recall in older age (Golchert et al. 2019) yet decline faster in global cognition and executive function than men (Levine et al. 2021). Moreover, stressful life events are associated with greater late-life memory decline in women but not in men (Munro et al. 2019), suggesting that sex may modify the cognitive effects of stress.

Prior research links housing conditions to cognitive health. Better housing quality predicts higher executive function (Vaid 2024), and housing distress is associated with increased cognitive impairment risk (Wilkinson et al. 2026). However, whether housing stress causally contributes to cognitive decline remains unclear (Walsh et al. 2025). Few studies assess episodic and working memory separately, and sex differences in these effects remain poorly characterized. This study examines the effect of ongoing housing stress on episodic and working memory trajectories among U.S. adults aged 50 and older. We also examine whether sex modifies this association, given that women may experience faster stress-related cognitive decline. We hypothesize that greater housing stress accelerates cognitive decline in both domains, with stronger effects among women than men.

Method

Data Collection

The Health and Retirement Study (HRS) is a nationally representative cohort of U.S. adults aged 50 and older, following approximately 20,000 participants across the United States over time. (Sonnega et al. 2014) The initial HRS cohort included individuals born between 1931 and 1941, who were first interviewed in 1992 (wave 1). (Bugliari et al. 2025) Core HRS interviews have been conducted by trained interviewers every two years since 1992. (Fisher and Ryan 2018) Before 2004, follow-up interviews were conducted primarily by telephone, with face-to-face interviews for respondents over the age of 80. (Sonnega et al. 2014) Beginning in 2006, HRS assigned half of the cohort to an enhanced face-to-face interview that included physical and biological measures and a psychosocial questionnaire, such that all participants received enhanced face-to-face interviews every four years. (Fisher and Ryan 2018) The HRS uses a multi-stage national probability sampling and adds a new cohort of individuals aged 51–56 every six years (e.g., in 1998, 2004, 2010, 2016). (Fisher and Ryan 2018; Juster and Suzman 1995; Willis 1999)

Analytic Sample

We used wave 8 (2006) as the baseline because the HRS began collecting ongoing housing stress data in that year. (Smith et al. 2023) Participants were followed for eight years across waves 8, 10, and 12. Only respondents were included; spouse or partner data were excluded because household members share the same housing environment, which could introduce correlated exposures within dyads. Wave 14 data were not included because non-missing exposure data at that wave represented only 2,800 observations, reflecting 62% missingness relative to the wave 8 sample (n = 7,422). Respondents who reported a doctor-diagnosed memory-related disease at baseline were excluded, as were respondents who lacked exposure or outcome data at baseline. Measurement

Exposure: Ongoing Housing Stress

Ongoing housing stress was measured by a single item assessing one of the ongoing chronic stressors designed to evaluate a respondent’s exposure to and perception of common chronic stressors. (Smith et al. 2023) Responses indicate whether a problem has lasted at least twelve months and how upsetting it has been, using four categories: “1 = No, didn’t happen,” “2 = Yes, but not upsetting,” “3 = Yes, somewhat upsetting,” and “4 = Yes, very upsetting.” The exposure was assessed at each wave (waves 8, 10, and 12) through self-report and handled as a single item, consistent with the HRS Psychosocial and Lifestyle Questionnaire user guide. (Smith et al. 2023)

Outcome: Cognitive Function

Cognitive function was assessed in two domains: episodic memory and working memory. Episodic memory was measured by total word recall, calculated as the sum of immediate and delayed word recall scores. For immediate word recall, the interviewer read a randomly assigned list of 10 nouns from four non-overlapping lists and asked the respondent to recall as many words as possible in any order; scores ranged from 0 to 10. (McCammon et al. 2023) Across waves, each respondent received a different list, and household partners were assigned lists so they would not receive the same list in the same or adjacent waves. Delayed word recall was assessed approximately five minutes after the immediate recall task, when respondents were asked to recall the previously presented nouns; scores also ranged from 0 to 10. (McCammon et al. 2023) Working memory was assessed by the serial 7’s task, in which the interviewer asked the respondent to subtract 7 from 100 five times consecutively; scores ranged from 0 to 5. (McCammon et al. 2023) Each subtraction was scored independently: for example, a respondent who answered the first item incorrectly but correctly subtracted 7 from their own answer for the remaining four trials received a score of 4. The interviewer did not repeat the respondent’s responses during the task. Because total word recall and serial 7’s are on different scales, both were standardized into z-scores, consistent with prior longitudinal cognitive research. (Andrade 2021; Song et al. 2025; Hahn and Lachman 2015)

Covariates

Covariates were selected and operationalized based on HRS documentation. (Bugliari et al. 2025) Age was calculated in years by dividing the number of months between the respondent’s birth date and interview date by 12. Sex (male or female) was treated as an effect measure modifier. Race and ethnicity were combined into five categories: Non-Hispanic White, Non-Hispanic Black, Hispanic, Non-Hispanic Other, and Other. Education was grouped into five categories: less than high school diploma, General Educational Development (GED), high school diploma, some college (less than a bachelor’s degree), and college and above. Total household income was used in nominal dollars. Current smoking status was coded as yes or no. We constructed an eight-condition comorbidity index in which each condition was coded 1 if the respondent ever reported a physician diagnosis in the current or any prior wave, and 0 otherwise; the eight conditions were hypertension, diabetes or high blood sugar, cancer or malignant tumor (excluding skin cancer), chronic lung disease (excluding asthma), heart disease (including heart attack, coronary heart disease, angina, congestive heart failure, or other heart problems), stroke or transient ischemic attack, emotional, nervous, or psychiatric problems, and arthritis or rheumatism. (Pan et al. 2023) The summed index ranged from 0 to 8, with higher scores indicating greater disease burden. Sex, race and ethnicity, and education were assessed at baseline; age, total household income, smoking status, and comorbidity index were treated as time-varying covariates and updated at each wave.

Statistical Analysis

Baseline characteristics of the analytic sample were summarized using means (SD) or medians (IQR) for continuous variables and counts (percentages) for categorical variables. For the main analysis, we used fixed-effects models to estimate standardized mean differences (coefficients) with 95% confidence intervals (CIs). Model 1 was an unadjusted (crude) model, and Model 2 was adjusted for age, total household income, and comorbidity index. Sex and education were not included as covariates because fixed-effects models control for observed and unobserved time-invariant characteristics by exploiting within-person variation. (Allison 2005; Quintana 2021) For the subgroup analysis, data were stratified by sex to examine effect measure modification; crude and adjusted models were applied separately to male and female subsamples.

Missing Data

For missing cognitive scores, we used the imputed cognition data provided by HRS. (McCammon et al. 2023) Missing values, refusals, and not-applicable responses were imputed using a multivariate, regression-based procedure conducted with Imputation and Variance Estimation (IVEware) software, drawing on a set of relevant demographic, health, and economic variables along with cognitive scores from the prior and current waves. “Don’t know” responses were treated as incorrect and were not imputed. Household income was used as reported in the RAND HRS dataset, where missing values were imputed by RAND using the unfolding brackets methodology. (Bugliari et al. 2025) Remaining missing data in the dataset were handled using multiple imputation (m = 20) with results pooled via Rubin’s rules.

Software

All analyses were conducted in R 4.5.1.
