Pragraph 1

  • (what is it?)

    • definitions

      • What is the housing stress

        • Housing is one of the causes of health disparities. (Swope and Hernández 2019)

        • In WHO’s framing for action on the social determinants of health in 2010, housing was categorized into intermediary determinants of health, which are more downstream factors than social determinants of health. (Solar and Irwin 2010)

        • The key elements of housing consist of affordability, suitability and security. (Mansour et al. 2022)

        • If those elements are not sustained, residents may face housing problems, such as homelessness, housing instability, and foreclosure.

        • In fact, the number of people facing housing problems are many and growing.

          • More than one third of community-living older Americans experience at least one form of housing insecurity (e.g. poor housing affordability, poor housing quality, and poor neighborhood quality). (Paredes et al. 2024)

          • In 2024, more than 700,000 people experienced homelessness in the United States, which was the largest number since 2007. (Sousa and Henry 2024)

        • In particular, older adults may be vulnerable to housing problems.

          • The number of older people, especially people aged 65+, is estimated to grow rapidly. (Culhane et al., n.d.)

        • Housing problems may also cause community level difficulties.

          • If the foreclosure rate of a community increases, the local government will reduce property tax as a revenue. (Apgar, Duda, and Gorey 2005)

          • Neighborhood eviction linked to community harm and higher neighborhood crime. (Schwartz et al. 2024)

    • key mechanisms, theories

      • Those housing problems affect mental for residents, and lead to stress. (Talmatzky et al. 2026; Singh et al. 2019; Dotsikas et al. 2023)

      • These housing stress may cause some adverse health outcomes.

        • One systematic review suggested that housing disadvantage may impact mental health. (Singh et al. 2019)

          • Experiencing housing problems was associated with depression. (Kingsbury et al. 2018)
          • Perceived risk of eviction associated with anxiety/depression and related medication use. (Acharya, Bhatta, and Dhakal 2022)

        • Another paper reported housing instability was associated with adverse cardiometabolic outcomes. (Gu, Faulkner, and Thorndike 2023)

      • Emerging evidence also suggests housing stress may accelerate memory decline and increase dementia risk.

        • For example, foreclosure may endanger older adults’ memory and may be a cause of a faster cognitive decline. (Kim and Schwartz 2024, Friedman2021–jz)

    • Given cognitive declines in mid-file and late life are associated with dementia and Alzheimer’s disease risks, it is important to detect factors of cognitive decline. (Dhana et al. 2025; Belleville et al. 2017)

    • Cognitive decline is not uniform across domains; episodic memory and working memory are especially relevant to dementia and Alzheimer’s disease.

      • Episodic memory, which assessed using immediate and delayed word recall, shows high diagnostic accuracy for Alzheimer’s disease and is widely studied as an early cognitive marker. [@Weissberger2017-vb]

      • Working memory, reflecting attentional control and central executive function, is also frequently impaired in Alzheimer’s disease. [@Kirova2015-ix; Stopford2012-bs]

      • Thus, these domains are clinically meaningful to evaluate whether housing-related stressors translate into dementia-relevant cognitive changes.

    • Effect measure modification

      • some papers show different responses of cognitive decline caused by chronic stress between men or women.

        • Women tend to score higher on immediate and delayed word recall among older age. (Golchert et al. 2019)

        • As for speed of cognitive decline, women may have faster declines in global cognition and executive function, compared with men. (Levine et al. 2021)

        • Also, recently occurring stressful life events reported was associated with greater memory decline later life in women but not in men. (Munro et al. 2019)

        • question and answer

          • This item was one of the items of ongoing chronic stressors, which wereused to assess a Respondent’s exposure and perception of common chronic stressors.

          • Responses indicate if a problem has lasted at least twelve months and how upsetting each problem has been using one of four categories, “1=No, didn’t happen,” “2=Yes, but not upsetting,” “3=Yes, somewhat upsetting,” or “4=Yes, very upsetting.” Items are scored 1-4 by HRS.

      • Episodic memory
        • Immediate Word Recall + Delayed Word Recall
          • total_recall = immediate + delayed
          • z_total_recall = (total_recall − mean(total_recall))/SD(total_recall)
          • Immediate Word Recall
            • measurement
              • The cognition variables are taken directly from the HRS imputations of cognitive functioning.
              • For Waves 14 forward, some cognition measures collected via Web interviews differed in their implementation, measurement, or collection method. Due to these differences, these cognition variables are separated into two measures: one ending in [P] for Face-to-Face/Telephone interviews and one ending in [W] for Web interviews. For some variables, only the [P] for Face-to-Face/Telephone interviews measure is provided.
              • The interviewer read a list of 10 nouns (e.g., lake, car, army, etc.) to the respondent, and asked the respondent to recall as many words as possible from the list in any order. The interviewer read one of four possible lists of 10 nouns to the respondent. The lists did not overlap in word content. In addition, the initial list was randomly assigned to the respondent, although the assignment was made longitudinally such that each respondent was assigned a different set of words in each of four successive waves of data collection. The assignment was also made so that two respondents in the same household (i.e., spouses or partners of one another) were not assigned the same set of words in the same or adjacent waves. (McCammon et al. 2023)
            • scoring
              • Count of number of words that were recalled correctly.
              • Count ranges from 0-10 for all waves from 8 to 13.
          • Delayed word recall
            • Measurement
              • After approximately 5 minutes of asking other survey questions (e.g., depression, and cognition items including backwards count, and serial 7’s) the respondent was asked to recall the nouns previously presented as part of the immediate recall task. (McCammon et al. 2023)
              • The questions asked between administration of the immediate word recall and delayed word recall tasks varied to some degree across survey waves.
            • scoring
              • Count of number of words that were recalled correctly.
              • Count ranges from 0-10 for all waves from 8 to 13.
      • Working memory
        • Serial 7’s
          • The interviewer asked the respondent to subtract 7 from 100, and continue subtracting 7 from each subsequent number for a total of five trials. It was up to the respondent to remember the value from the prior subtraction, such that the interviewer did not repeat the difference said by the respondent after each trial. (McCammon et al. 2023)
        • Scoring
          • Scoring is a count of the number of correct subtractions among the five trials (0-5). Each subtraction was scored independently.
          • For example, if a respondent made a mistake on the first subtraction (e.g., reported 92 instead of 93) but gave correct answers for each subsequent subtraction (using 92 as a starting point and answering 85 for the second subtraction), he/she would receive a score of 4.
      • Imputation
        • Before multiple imputation, HRS data offered imputed cognition data.
        • Values were imputed to replace missing values, refusals (RF), and any not applicable (NA) response using a multivariate, regression-based procedure using Imputation and Variance Estimation (IVEware) software.
        • A combination of relevant demographic, health, and economic variables, as well as prior and current wave cognitive variables to perform the imputations.
        • Don’t Know (DK) responses were coded as incorrect and were not imputed.
        • We used imputed cognition data as is, then NA cognition data were imputed by multiple imputation.
